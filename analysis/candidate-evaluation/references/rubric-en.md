# Candidate evaluation rubric

Full scoring anchors for the nine default dimensions used in `candidate-evaluation`.

Scoring scale: 1 to 5 per dimension.
- 1: Disqualifier.
- 2: Below bar.
- 3: Bar. Hire if other dimensions are not below.
- 4: Strong. Accelerate.
- 5: Exceptional. Rare, and worth calling out.

---

## 1. Judgment under ambiguity

How the candidate navigates when the right answer is not given, when information is incomplete, when stakeholders disagree.

- **1.** Freezes or picks the first option offered without questioning it. Waits for authority to decide. Treats ambiguity as a problem with the question.
- **3.** Names the uncertainty. Asks the clarifying question. Picks a path with a reason that survives pushback, and adjusts when given new information.
- **5.** Decides cleanly under ambiguity without inventing certainty. Names tradeoffs. Revises without losing confidence. Produces a decision others can act on, not a defense of their own caution.

## 2. Delivery track record

Evidence of having shipped real things under real conditions.

- **1.** Describes roles in generalities. Cannot name a specific deliverable they personally owned. Attributes wins to teams without specifying their role.
- **3.** Names specific shipped work with their role, the timeline, and the tradeoffs made. Can describe what almost went wrong and how it was handled.
- **5.** Delivers a sharp causal account of past work, including the decisions that were not obvious at the time. Can name what they would do differently and why.

## 3. Technical depth vs breadth

The correct mix depends on the role. Score against the role-specific target, not an abstract ideal.

- **1.** Neither deep nor broad where the role needs it. Surface answers to concrete technical questions. Breadth is buzzword-level.
- **3.** Meets the target mix for the role. Can produce substance in the required areas. Breadth is informed, not name-dropped.
- **5.** Deep where depth is required, demonstrates awareness of adjacent systems, picks up unfamiliar areas in real time during the interview.

## 4. Ownership signal

Whether the candidate treats work as theirs or as someone else's.

- **1.** Uses "they" to describe their own team's work. Distances from failures. Takes credit only for things that worked.
- **3.** Uses "I" and "we" accurately. Names what was theirs, what was shared, and what was someone else's. Treats responsibility as theirs even when the outcome was mixed.
- **5.** Volunteers ownership of things they could plausibly have blamed on others. Explains their accountability even when the situation was structurally unfair. Avoids martyr framing.

## 5. Failure fluency

How cleanly the candidate discusses things that went wrong.

- **1.** Denies failures. Reframes every mistake as a learning experience without the learning. Describes past decisions as correct even when outcomes were bad.
- **3.** Discusses failures directly. Names their role. Can describe what they would do differently now, specifically.
- **5.** Brings up failures unprompted when relevant. Distinguishes decision quality from outcome quality. Shows updated models from past failures operating in current thinking.

## 6. Scope compression

Ability to cut work to what matters, not just to expand to fill available time.

- **1.** Describes scoping only in terms of what they added. Treats cutting as losing. Cannot name what they deliberately did not build.
- **3.** Names specific scope cuts and why. Describes the tradeoff consciously. Can defend the cut without repudiating it later.
- **5.** Treats cutting as a primary tool. Describes a pattern of shipping less than was on the roadmap and winning. Can name the decision frame they used to cut.

## 7. Communication density

Signal per sentence. Overwriting is expensive; underwriting hides.

- **1.** Padding dominates. Hard to find the claim in the paragraph. Or so terse that context is missing.
- **3.** Answers the question asked. Adds context only where it matters. Does not repeat themselves to fill silence.
- **5.** High signal consistently. Makes complex ideas precise in few words. Adjusts density to audience without losing substance.

## 8. Political literacy

Reading the room, anticipating stakeholder reactions, not being naive about authority and interest.

- **1.** Describes past disagreements as either other people being irrational or themselves being naive. Treats stakeholder objections as obstacles, not information.
- **3.** Anticipates where pushback will come from and why. Distinguishes between what people say and what they want. Adjusts approach without losing position.
- **5.** Operates naturally across technical, commercial, and political frames. Names the interest underneath an objection. Moves ideas forward without either steamrolling or flattening.

## 9. Learning velocity

Pace of absorbing new domains. Not intelligence; pace.

- **1.** Learns at the speed of courses and documentation. Struggles to pick up unfamiliar terrain in real time.
- **3.** Absorbs adjacent domains at a normal professional pace. Asks good questions. Converts answers into working models.
- **5.** Learns in real time inside the interview. Converts the interviewer's pushback into a sharper answer within the same conversation.

---

## Role-specific overrides

For each role, note which of the nine carry more weight and which can be traded down. Add role-specific dimensions if needed. Examples:

- **IC staff engineer.** Upweight depth, judgment, failure fluency. Trade down political literacy.
- **Engineering manager.** Upweight political literacy, ownership, communication density. Trade down depth beyond a functional minimum.
- **Customer-facing PM.** Add "customer register". Upweight communication density and political literacy.
- **Founding engineer.** Upweight scope compression and learning velocity. Depth and breadth both required.

Overrides are written before interviews. Changes after the fact invalidate the process.
